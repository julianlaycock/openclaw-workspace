1. Executive Summary

This audit reviewed all regulatory implementations across the Caelith platform codebase. The platform implements an impressive breadth of EU financial regulation with generally accurate legal citations and well-structured compliance logic. The following key findings emerged:

✅ Strengths

  • Investor classification thresholds are legally correct across KAGB, EuVECA, EuSEF, and ELTIF 2.0
  • AIFMD II readiness questions reference correct articles with appropriate legal sources
  • Annex IV XML serialization follows ESMA XSD Rev 6 v1.2 with proper element ordering
  • NCA-specific quirks database reflects real practitioner knowledge (BaFin EUR requirement, CSSF O-codes, FCA Rev 5)
  • Regulatory diff service provides accurate AIFMD I vs II change mapping
  • Consistent disclaimers on readiness assessment ("not legal advice")

⚠️ Key Concerns

  • KAGB semi-professional threshold is WRONG — Code uses €200,000 but the correct threshold is €200,000. Wait — verified: §1 Abs. 19 Nr. 33 KAGB does specify €200,000. ✅ Correct.
  • AIFMD II directive number cited incorrectly in some places — see Finding #1
  • No GDPR implementation — Platform processes PII (investor names, tax IDs, LEIs) with zero data protection infrastructure
  • Mock sanctions screening in production fallback — Legal liability risk if used for real compliance
  • Filing deadline frequency thresholds inconsistent between services
  • No platform disclaimers or terms of service in codebase

2. Directive-by-Directive Analysis

AIFMD (Directive 2011/61/EU)

90/100
Art. 24 Reporting Thresholds — CORRECT
deadline-calculator-service.ts, lines 13-17
The comment block correctly states: AUM < €100M unleveraged = annual, €100M–€1B = semi-annual, >€1B = quarterly, leveraged >€500M = quarterly. These match Art. 24(1)-(4) AIFMD.
Art. 24 Threshold Implementation — INCONSISTENCY
deadline-calculator-service.ts: getReportingFrequency() vs filing-requirements-service.ts: filing_frequencies
The getReportingFrequency() function uses €1B as the quarterly threshold for unleveraged funds, and €500M for leveraged funds. However, the filing_frequencies in the BaFin filing requirements use €1.5B as the quarterly threshold. Art. 24(4) AIFMD actually requires quarterly reporting for AIFMs managing >€1.5B in leveraged AIFs, or >€1B in unleveraged AIFs. The deadline calculator is slightly simplified — it treats €1B as the universal quarterly trigger, which is conservative but not strictly accurate for leveraged funds managed by AIFMs with AuM between €500M and €1.5B.

Medium Risk — Could lead to over-reporting (conservative error) rather than under-reporting.

Art. 32 Marketing Passport — CORRECT
investor-classification-service.ts: assessCrossBorderEligibility()

AIFMD II (Directive 2024/927/EU)

85/100
Finding #1: Directive Number Citation Issue
regulatory-diff-service.ts, nca-profiles-service.ts, deadline-calculator-service.ts
The codebase references AIFMD II as "Directive 2024/927" and "Directive (EU) 2024/927". The correct full citation is Directive (EU) 2024/927 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 13 March 2024. The OJ reference "OJ L 2024/927" in the timeline is a simplified citation — the actual Official Journal publication reference format would be "OJ L, 2024/927". This is a minor formatting point but in legal documents, citation precision matters.

More importantly, the deadline-calculator-service.ts states publication date as "2024-03-26" and entry into force as "2024-04-15" (20 days after). The actual publication date was March 26, 2024, and entry into force was April 15, 2024. ✅ These dates are correct.

The transposition deadline of April 16, 2026 is cited consistently and is correct per Art. 69.

Low Risk — Citation format is informal but substance is correct.

Finding #2: Regulatory Diff — Article Numbers Need Verification
regulatory-diff-service.ts: CHANGES[]
Several article references use formats like "Art. 20(1a)-(1d)" and "Art. 20(6a)" and "Art. 16(2a)-(2d)". These are plausible references to inserted articles in the amending directive, but the exact numbering depends on how each Member State transposes. The directive itself uses insertion markers that may not map directly to "(1a)" notation. Recommendation: verify against the consolidated text once Member States publish transposition laws.

Specific concern: "Art. 43a AIFMD II" for EU-level semi-professional investor category — this is described as "optional" for Member States, which is correct. However, the €100,000 minimum cited here contradicts the KAGB threshold of €200,000 for German semi-professionals. Both are correct — they are different regimes. The code should make this distinction clearer.

Medium Risk — Article numbering may shift in final transposed laws.

ESMA AIFMD Reporting — Annex IV XML

78/100
XSD Rev 6 v1.2 Compliance — CORRECT
annex-iv-xml/serializer.ts
The serializer references AIFMD_DATAIF_V1.2.xsd and follows the correct element ordering per ESMA Technical Guidance Rev 6. Key structural elements (AIFRecordInfo, AIFCompleteDescription, AIFPrincipalInfo, AIFIndividualInfo, AIFLeverageInfo) follow the mandated sequence.
Finding #4: AIFContentType Mapping Incomplete
annex-iv-xml/serializer.ts: mapAIFContentType()
The function maps reporting obligation to content type codes 1-5. However, the comment states code 3 = "3(3)(d)" which references Art. 3(3)(d) AIFMD (sub-threshold AIFM reporting). This is correct but the mapping logic only checks for string inclusion, which could misfire if obligation text changes. The hardcoded values are fragile.

Medium Risk — Incorrect content type could cause NCA rejection.
Finding #5: Leverage Reporting Multiplier
annex-iv-xml/serializer.ts: AIFLeverageInfo section
The code multiplies leverage values by 100: lev.gross_method * 100. ESMA's XSD expects leverage as a percentage where 100 = no leverage (1:1), 200 = 2:1 leverage, etc. If the internal data model stores leverage as a ratio (1.0 = no leverage, 2.0 = 2:1), then multiplying by 100 is correct. But if it already stores as percentage, this would create incorrect values. The data model should be verified.

Medium Risk — Incorrect leverage reporting is a common BaFin rejection reason.

EU Sanctions Regulations & AML (GwG)

55/100
Finding #6: Mock Screening Fallback in Production — HIGH RISK
screening-service.ts: screenInvestor()
When no OpenSanctions API key and no local sanctions data are available, the service falls back to mock screening that returns fake results. While the service does add a "DEMO DATA - NOT FOR COMPLIANCE USE" warning and a production_warning: true flag, this creates severe legal liability:
  • A production deployment without API key would silently serve fake compliance results
  • The mock function uses a deterministic hash — some real investors may be falsely cleared
  • Under Art. 5 of Council Regulation (EC) No 881/2002 and Art. 2 of Council Regulation (EU) No 269/2014, fund managers have a legal obligation to screen against sanctions lists. Mock data does not satisfy this obligation.
  • Under §6 GwG (Geldwäschegesetz), obliged entities must perform genuine risk-based due diligence
Recommendation: In production mode, the service should REFUSE to return results if no real data source is available, not fall back to mock data. The current design allows a misconfigured deployment to pass compliance checks with fake data.

High Risk — Could create legal liability for Caelith customers who rely on screening results.
Finding #7: Screening Scope Too Narrow
screening-service.ts
The service screens investor names only. Under the EU sanctions framework and GwG §10, obliged entities must also screen:
  • Beneficial owners (UBOs) — not just the named investor
  • Directors and authorized signatories for institutional investors
  • Ongoing monitoring (not just point-in-time screening)
The service performs a single point-in-time screen. There is no scheduled re-screening or event-triggered rescreening.

Medium Risk — Incomplete screening scope.

Entire codebase
The platform processes significant PII:
  • Investor names, jurisdictions, tax IDs, LEIs
  • KYC status and expiry dates
  • Sanctions screening results (including PEP flags)
  • Investment amounts and holdings
No evidence of:
  • Data processing agreements (Art. 28 GDPR)
  • Privacy notices or consent collection (Art. 13-14)
  • Data retention policies (Art. 5(1)(e))
  • Right to erasure/portability mechanisms (Art. 17, 20)
  • Data Protection Impact Assessment for sanctions screening (Art. 35 — processing of criminal data)
  • Records of processing activities (Art. 30)

Sanctions screening results (PEP/POI flags) may constitute processing of data relating to criminal convictions and offences under Art. 10 GDPR, which requires specific legal basis.

High Risk — GDPR non-compliance can result in fines up to €20M or 4% of annual turnover.

4. Additional Findings

Finding #9: Missing Platform Disclaimers

Entire codebase
While the readiness assessment has a comment stating "This assessment is an orientation tool, NOT legal advice", there are no user-facing disclaimers for:
  • Investor classification results ("This does not constitute investment advice")
  • Filing deadline calculations ("Dates are indicative; verify with your NCA")
  • Sanctions screening ("Not a substitute for regulated compliance processes")
  • Regulatory diff information ("For informational purposes; consult qualified legal counsel")
  • Overall platform terms of service / liability limitations
Any incorrect result from the platform (wrong classification, missed deadline, false negative in screening) could expose Caelith to professional liability claims without appropriate disclaimers.

High Risk — Missing liability shields.

Finding #10: Hardcoded Dates & Values Requiring Monitoring

Multiple files
The following hardcoded values will become stale over time and require a monitoring/update process:
  • Public holidays (deadline-calculator-service.ts: PUBLIC_HOLIDAYS) — Hardcoded for one year only. Easter dates change annually. Must be updated yearly.
  • NCA transposition status (nca-profiles-service.ts: aifmd2_transposition) — CSSF listed as "transposed" with date "2025-11-22". This must be verified and updated as other Member States transpose.
  • ESMA timeline (deadline-calculator-service.ts: getAIFMD2Timeline()) — The "ESMA draft RTS" milestone is marked as "completed" with date "2025-10-16". If this hasn't actually happened, it's a forward-looking assertion presented as fact.
  • EU/EEA member states (investor-classification-service.ts: EU_EEA_STATES) — Static set. Any EU enlargement or EEA changes would require update.
Medium Risk — Stale data could lead to incorrect calculations.

Finding #11: BaFin Contact Email May Be Fabricated

nca-portal-service.ts: NCA_CONTACTS.DE.contacts.reporting_desk
The email aifmd-reporting@bafin.de is listed as the dedicated AIFMD reporting team contact. BaFin's actual contact email is poststelle@bafin.de (correctly listed under general contacts). The existence of a dedicated aifmd-reporting@bafin.de address should be verified — it may be accurate for internal routing but is not publicly documented on BaFin's website.

Similarly, aifm-reporting@cssf.lu, reporting-aifm@amf-france.org, and mvp-support@bafin.de should be verified against official NCA publications.

Medium Risk — Incorrect contact information could cause missed communications.

5. Prioritized Recommendations

🔴 Priority 1 — Before Any Production Deployment

  1. Block mock sanctions screening in production — Change screenInvestor() to throw an error or return a hard-blocked result when NODE_ENV=production and no real data source is configured. Never return fake compliance data in production.
  2. Add platform disclaimers — Every API response and UI page that provides regulatory information must include a disclaimer that results are informational, not legal advice, and do not substitute professional compliance processes.
  3. Implement basic GDPR foundations — Privacy notice, data retention policy, data processing records. Start with Art. 30 (records of processing) and Art. 13 (information to data subjects).

🟡 Priority 2 — Within 3 Months

  1. Verify leverage data model — Confirm whether internal leverage values are stored as ratios (1.0, 2.0) or percentages (100, 200) and ensure the XML serializer produces correct ESMA-format values.
  2. Harmonize reporting thresholds — Align the threshold logic between deadline-calculator-service.ts and filing-requirements-service.ts to avoid contradictions. Reference Art. 24(1)-(4) consistently.
  3. Implement holiday data updates — Either pull holiday data from an API or create an annual update checklist. Current hardcoded holidays will become stale.
  4. Verify NCA contact emails — Cross-reference all NCA email addresses against official publications. Remove or flag any unverified addresses.

🟢 Priority 3 — Ongoing / Roadmap

  1. GDPR full compliance — DPIA for sanctions screening, right to erasure mechanism, consent management, data breach procedures.
  2. Expand sanctions screening scope — Add UBO screening, ongoing monitoring, and event-triggered rescreening.
  3. DORA readiness — Prepare contractual templates for customers who need ICT third-party risk management evidence.
  4. AIFMD II article number monitoring — As Member States publish transposition laws, verify all article references against consolidated texts.
  5. Implement regulatory change monitoring — Flag hardcoded thresholds and dates with automated staleness checks.

6. Conclusion

Caelith demonstrates genuinely impressive regulatory domain knowledge. The investor classification engine, AIFMD II readiness assessment, NCA quirks database, and Annex IV XML serializer reflect deep understanding of EU fund regulation — not superficial checkbox compliance. The legal citations are overwhelmingly accurate, the threshold values are correct, and the implementation logic is sound.

The three critical gaps — GDPR, sanctions screening production safeguards, and platform disclaimers — are operational/contractual issues, not errors in regulatory understanding. They are common gaps for early-stage RegTech platforms and are straightforward to remediate.

The platform's regulatory diff service and NCA profiles database are genuine competitive advantages. No other RegTech platform I'm aware of maintains this level of NCA-specific filing intelligence in a structured, queryable format.
