Part 1
Assumption audit

Every strategic decision we’ve made was based on assumptions. Some were validated. Some were wrong. Some were never tested. This is the honest accounting.

Wrong Fund managers (KVGs) are our primary buyer

We assumed KVG Geschäftsführer and compliance officers would buy a compliance tool directly. They don’t. They outsource compliance operations to fund administrators and Service-KVGs. The decision-maker for compliance tooling is the fund admin, not the fund manager.

Evidence: Cold outreach to 10+ KVGs produced 1 response: "We outsource these services." Zero demos booked from fund manager outreach. Egor Tarakanov (Montold) responded positively but is a fund admin/service provider, not a fund manager.
Revised hypothesis Fund administrators and Service-KVGs are the primary buyer. They manage compliance for 10–50+ funds each. One deal replaces 50 individual KVG outreach attempts.

Risky The April 16 AIFMD II deadline creates urgency to buy

We assumed the deadline would drive purchases. In practice, most AIFMs have been aware of this deadline for 2+ years and have either already prepared (via existing providers) or decided to wait and see. The deadline creates awareness, not conversion.

Evidence: Zero purchases driven by deadline urgency. Fund managers who care already have solutions. Those who don’t care won’t be scared into buying from an unknown startup 6 weeks before enforcement.
Revised hypothesis Post-deadline enforcement actions (fines, audit findings) will create more urgency than the deadline itself. The real buying window is Q3–Q4 2026, not Q1. But we need pipeline NOW to convert then.

Risky Our product is good enough to sell itself via demo

The product is technically impressive: XSD validation, GLEIF integration, sanctions screening, audit trail. But "technically impressive" doesn’t matter if the buyer doesn’t trust the vendor. A compliance officer won’t evaluate the product if they don’t believe the company will exist in 12 months.

Evidence: Demo login works. Product features are live. Zero demo conversions. The blocker isn’t the product — it’s the trust gap (see credibility section).
Revised hypothesis The product needs to be experienced in context (pilot with real fund data) by someone who evaluates tools professionally (fund admin ops team), not pitched via cold email to someone who doesn’t evaluate tools (KVG GF).

Wrong Cold email outreach to compliance officers will generate demos

We sent emails to KVG compliance officers and Geschäftsführer. Response rate: effectively 0%. Compliance officers at small KVGs don’t open emails from unknown startups. They’re risk-averse by profession. Our email subject line competes with regulatory circulars, not SaaS pitches.

Evidence: 10+ cold emails to Tier 1 Immobilien-KVGs. 1 response ("we outsource"). 0 demos. LinkedIn outreach: minimal engagement.
Revised hypothesis Fund admin operations teams evaluate tools as part of their job. Cold email to "Head of Fund Operations" or "Head of Regulatory Reporting" at a fund admin will get a higher response rate than "Compliance Officer" at a KVG. Target: 10–15% response rate (vs. ~2% current).

Valid AIFMD II compliance is painful and manual

This is confirmed. Annex IV filing is genuinely manual, error-prone, and time-consuming. The BaFin MVP Portal is clunky. XSD validation is done by hand or not at all. Fund admins hate this process.

Evidence: Every industry source confirms this. anevis exists because the problem is real. Montold’s positive response confirms pain. The readiness check engagement (people take it) confirms awareness.

Valid XSD validation and Annex IV automation have real technical value

Our XML generation passes ESMA Rev 6 validation. open-annex-iv is the only open-source Annex IV library. GLEIF integration works in real-time. Sanctions screening runs against 6,863 entities with fuzzy matching. This is real, not vaporware.

Evidence: Live demo works. 231 tests passing. XSD validation confirmed against ESMA schema. GLEIF API returns live data. Finch Capital was impressed by the technical demo.

Untested Fund admins will pay €200–500/fund/year for filing automation

We’ve assumed per-fund pricing works. We haven’t validated this with a single fund admin. They may want flat pricing, seat-based pricing, or volume discounts. The pricing model is entirely theoretical.

How to test In the first 3 fund admin conversations, ask: "If this saved your team 60% of filing time per fund, what would that be worth to you per fund per year?" Don’t pitch a price — let them anchor first.

Untested Fund admins will pilot with an unknown startup

Fund admins are more pragmatic than fund managers about evaluating tools. But they still need to trust you with sensitive fund data. A 90-day free pilot de-risks this, but we haven’t confirmed anyone will accept even a free pilot.

How to test Offer free pilot to 5 mid-tier fund admins. Success = 1 accepts within 4 weeks. If 0/5 accept, the trust gap exists at the fund admin level too, and we need a different entry point (e.g., open-source adoption first).

Untested The open-source library creates inbound pipeline

open-annex-iv is on npm. But npm downloads ≠ fund admin leads. The assumption that developers at fund admins will find it, use it, and escalate to their compliance team is unproven. Most fund admin IT teams may not use npm at all.

How to test Track npm downloads weekly. Write 2 technical blog posts targeting fund admin dev teams. If downloads stay <50/week after 4 weeks of promotion, the developer-led growth thesis doesn’t work for this market.

Risky AML/AMLR is a growth engine for Caelith

The AML screening space is dominated by well-funded competitors (Sphinx $7.1M, ComplyAdvantage $100M+, Sumsub $80M). Caelith entering AML as a primary value prop is not viable. However, fund-specific AML screening as a FEATURE inside the compliance platform (not the product) is defensible.

Evidence: 5+ AML-focused companies with $200M+ combined funding. AMLR July 2027 creates new AMLA reporting requirements (not yet built by anyone), but the screening layer is commoditized.
Revised position AML screening = table stakes feature. AMLR reporting to AMLA = future opportunity (monitor, don’t build yet). Primary moat = AIFMD II filing pipeline + cross-NCA knowledge.

Part 3
The credibility gap — and how to close it

You’re asking compliance professionals to trust their regulatory filings to a company with 11 LinkedIn followers. Here’s exactly what’s missing and how to fix each gap.

Gap 1 No legal entity

Caelith has no UG or GmbH. Fund admins can’t sign a contract with a Freiberufler for regulatory software. This is a hard blocker for any pilot agreement.

Fix (Week 1) Incorporate UG (haftungsbeschränkt). Cost: ~€300–500 + notary. Timeline: 2–3 weeks to Handelsregister. Do NOT wait for Gründungszuschuss — BAfA meeting is March 5, ask explicitly if UG registration is compatible.

Gap 2 No social proof

11 LinkedIn followers. No customer logos. No testimonials. No case studies. No press. A fund admin Googling "Caelith" finds a nice website and nothing else.

Fix (Weeks 1–4) (a) Post 3x/week on LinkedIn — not "AIFMD II deadline" fear content but "fund admin efficiency" value content. (b) Get Montold’s permission to use their name after pilot. (c) Write 2 guest posts on fund admin industry blogs. (d) Aim for 100 LinkedIn followers by Week 4 (currently 11).

Gap 3 No advisory board

No recognizable names associated with Caelith. A retired BaFin examiner or ex-compliance head on the advisory board gives instant permission to take the meeting.

Fix (Weeks 2–6) Identify 3 candidates: (a) retired BaFin/CSSF official, (b) ex-Head of Compliance at a German KVG, (c) fund admin industry veteran. Offer: title + small equity/advisory fee + input on product direction. Ask Finch Capital for introductions.

Gap 4 No accelerator / VC backing

Campus Founders Batch 4 application is pending. Finch Capital conversation is active. Neither has converted yet. Until one does, you have no institutional signal.

Fix (ongoing) Follow up on Campus Founders. Push Finch Capital conversation toward a term sheet or explicit "no." If both fall through, apply to: EXIST (HTW Berlin contact exists), IBB Berlin, Prototype Fund (April round). One "yes" changes everything.

Gap 5 Content doesn’t target the new buyer

Current content (landing page, blog, readiness check) targets fund managers. The readiness check asks "is YOUR fund ready?" — but our buyer is the person who MAKES funds ready. The messaging needs to flip.

Fix (Week 2) Create a "Fund Admin Efficiency Calculator" landing page. Input: number of funds, hours per filing cycle. Output: hours saved with Caelith. This captures fund admin leads and speaks their language.

Part 8
Kill criteria — when to pivot or stop

Every startup needs pre-defined exit conditions. These aren’t pessimism — they’re discipline. If we hit these thresholds, we stop, reassess, and decide whether to pivot the approach or pivot the company.

❌ Hard kill: 0 pilots by June 30

If after 4 months of fund admin outreach (30+ emails, content marketing, LinkedIn effort), zero fund admins have agreed to even a free pilot, the market is not ready for this product from this company. Options: (a) pivot to pure open-source + consulting, (b) find a co-founder with fund admin industry connections, (c) wind down and preserve remaining runway.

❌ Hard kill: 0 revenue by September 30

If a pilot converts but can’t convert to paid, or if all pilots stall, the product has usage but not purchase intent. Options: (a) change pricing model dramatically (freemium?), (b) pivot to infrastructure-only (API licensing), (c) open-source everything and monetize via services.

⚠ Soft pivot: <5% email response rate after 30 emails

If fund admins don’t respond either, the problem isn’t the buyer segment — it’s the channel or the message. Actions: (a) try LinkedIn DMs instead of email, (b) try industry events/conferences, (c) try channel partners (compliance consultants who serve fund admins).

⚠ Soft pivot: Pilot customer doesn’t see value

If the pilot runs and the fund admin says "nice but we’ll stick with Excel" — the product doesn’t solve a painful enough problem. Actions: (a) double down on the pain point they DO have (maybe it’s not Annex IV but sanctions screening?), (b) add BaFin portal submission to make it end-to-end, (c) rebuild for a different workflow.

Part 9
Competitive moat — what we own vs. what’s crowded

Not everything is defensible. Here’s an honest map of where Caelith has a moat, where it’s competing on commodity ground, and where it shouldn’t compete at all.

Moat AIFMD II Annex IV filing pipeline

XSD-validated XML generation against ESMA Rev 6. open-annex-iv on npm. Nobody else has an open-source Annex IV library. The filing pipeline (data → validation → XML → portal submission) is technically complex and domain-specific. anevis is the only real competitor, and they’re enterprise/managed service, not self-service/mid-market.

Moat Cross-NCA knowledge

BaFin MVP Portal, CSSF eDesk, AMF GECO — different portals, different requirements, different deadlines. This knowledge takes months to build and requires deep regulatory expertise to maintain. Every new NCA we support widens the moat.

Moat Open-source community positioning

open-annex-iv is the ONLY open-source Annex IV toolkit. NGI Zero grant application submitted. If adopted by developers at fund admins, it creates gravity toward the commercial product. This is unique — no competitor has an open-source strategy.

Commodity Sanctions screening

Fuzzy matching against EU/UN sanctions lists is a feature, not a product. ComplyAdvantage, Refinitiv, LexisNexis all do this better. Our screening is good enough as a bundled feature but cannot be a standalone selling point.

Commodity LEI verification

GLEIF API is public. Anyone can query it. Our value is integrating it into the filing workflow (auto-verify before XML generation), not the lookup itself.

Don’t compete AML/KYC as primary product

Sphinx ($7.1M), ComplyAdvantage ($100M+), Sumsub ($80M), Onfido (acquired). Entering this space as a primary value prop is suicide. Keep AML as a feature. Don’t build an AML product.

Don’t compete General compliance dashboard

AI commoditizes dashboards. We built ours in 10 days. Anyone can build a compliance dashboard with Claude/GPT. The dashboard is a delivery mechanism, not the moat. Infrastructure (filing pipelines, XSD validation, NCA portal agents) is the moat.

The one-sentence moat: Caelith is the only company that can take fund data in, validate it against ESMA XSD, and submit it directly to BaFin/CSSF/AMF — with an open-source schema library creating developer gravity. Everything else is a feature that supports this pipeline.
