Caelith vs FundApps: AIFMD Annex IV Reporting Compared (2026)

March 2026 · 8 min read · Comparison

FundApps is one of the most established names in regulatory reporting software, with thousands of clients globally and a reputation built over more than a decade. Caelith is a newcomer built on a fundamentally different architectural principle: autonomous agents instead of human-supervised workflows.

This is a fair comparison. We're not going to claim FundApps is obsolete or that Caelith is perfect. Both solve the problem of AIFMD Annex IV reporting — but they do it in very different ways, and the right choice depends on your organization's priorities.

The Core Difference: Philosophy

FundApps is built around assisted workflow. The platform provides data aggregation, compliance checks, and reporting templates — but a human compliance officer reviews and approves every submission. FundApps excels at presenting complex regulatory data in a digestible format for expert review.

Caelith is built around autonomous agents. Software agents perform the entire filing process — data extraction, XML generation, validation, portal submission, confirmation monitoring — with humans monitoring for exceptions. Caelith excels at eliminating repetitive work from the compliance workflow entirely.

Neither approach is inherently "better" — they serve different philosophies about where the human should be in the loop.

When FundApps Makes Sense

FundApps is a better fit if you:

  • File across many jurisdictions globally — FundApps covers not just AIFMD but MiFID, EMIR, SFTR, REMIT, and dozens of other regimes. If you're a global asset manager filing in 10+ countries, the breadth of coverage is unmatched.
  • Prefer human review before submission — Some compliance officers are uncomfortable with fully automated filing. FundApps gives you aggregated data and templates, but the final "submit" button is always human-pressed.
  • Need battle-tested enterprise software — FundApps has been around since 2010, with a client list including some of the largest asset managers in the world. If your risk committee requires vendors with a long track record, this matters.
  • Want a comprehensive regulatory data platform — FundApps is more than just a filing tool. It's a compliance dashboard that centralizes regulatory obligations across multiple regimes. Useful for large, complex organizations.

When Caelith Makes Sense

Caelith is a better fit if you:

  • Want maximum automation — You believe the compliance officer's job is to oversee the process, not perform it. Caelith agents handle the repetitive steps so your team can focus on exceptions and strategic work.
  • File primarily with BaFin, CSSF, or other EU NCAs — Caelith is purpose-built for BaFin and CSSF, with direct portal integration. If your regulatory footprint is EU-focused, you don't need the global breadth FundApps offers.
  • Prefer transparent, usage-based pricing — FundApps operates on enterprise licensing with pricing that scales with AUM and user count. Caelith charges per fund per quarter — you know exactly what you're paying for.
  • Value API-first architecture — Caelith is built as an API from the ground up. If you're building a compliance tech stack and want programmatic control, Caelith integrates more naturally into modern workflows.
  • Are an early adopter comfortable with newer platforms — Caelith's agent-first approach is novel. If you're willing to trade a 15-year track record for cutting-edge automation and lower costs, this is the tradeoff.

Pricing Reality Check

Both companies approach pricing differently, making direct comparison tricky:

FundApps uses enterprise licensing. Pricing is not publicly disclosed and varies based on AUM, number of funds, number of users, and regulatory modules required. Based on public references and industry benchmarks, a mid-sized AIFM managing 10 funds can expect to pay €40,000–80,000+ annually. Larger managers with complex multi-jurisdiction needs can pay significantly more.

Caelith uses transparent, usage-based pricing: per fund per reporting period (quarterly, semi-annual, or annual). No hidden setup fees, no user-based pricing. A KVG filing Annex IV for 10 funds quarterly would pay approximately €15,000–30,000 annually, depending on complexity and data sources.

The Caelith model is more predictable and transparent. The FundApps model may offer better value for very large organizations leveraging the platform across dozens of regulatory regimes.

Automation Level: The Real Difference

This is where the two platforms diverge most sharply.

FundApps workflow:

  1. Data is uploaded to FundApps (via file or API)
  2. FundApps aggregates and maps data to regulatory templates
  3. Compliance officer reviews the pre-filled report
  4. Officer makes adjustments if needed
  5. Officer downloads the XML or PDF
  6. Officer manually uploads to NCA portal (BaFin MVP, CSSF eDesk, etc.)
  7. Officer monitors for confirmation receipt

The human is involved in steps 3-7. Estimated time per fund: 1–2 hours.

Caelith workflow:

  1. Agent connects to data sources (API or file)
  2. Agent generates and validates XML
  3. Agent submits to NCA portal
  4. Agent monitors for confirmation
  5. Agent logs to audit trail and notifies compliance team

The human is involved in step 0 (setup) and exception handling only. Estimated time per fund: <15 minutes (mostly review).

For a compliance team managing 10 funds quarterly, that's the difference between 10-20 hours of work per quarter (FundApps) vs 2-3 hours (Caelith). Over a year, that's 80 hours of saved compliance officer time — roughly 2 weeks of full-time work.

The Honest Verdict

FundApps is the safe, established choice. If you're a large asset manager with complex global regulatory obligations, a conservative risk appetite, and the budget for enterprise software, FundApps is a proven solution.

Caelith is the efficiency play. If you're an EU-focused AIFM that wants to eliminate manual filing work, values transparent pricing, and is comfortable with a newer platform built on modern architecture, Caelith delivers materially lower costs and time savings.

Both are legitimate solutions. The question is: do you want a platform that helps humans file, or one that files autonomously while humans supervise?
